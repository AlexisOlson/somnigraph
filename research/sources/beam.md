# BEAM: Beyond a Million Tokens -- Benchmarking and Enhancing Long-Term Memory in LLMs -- Analysis

*Phase 15, 2026-03-06. Analysis of arXiv:2510.27246 (ICLR 2026).*

---

## 1. Paper Overview

**Paper**: Mohammad Tavakoli, Alireza Salemi, Carrie Ye, Mohamed Abdalla, Hamed Zamani, J. Ross Mitchell. University of Alberta and University of Massachusetts Amherst. ICLR 2026 (confirmed via GitHub repo tag). arXiv:2510.27246. v1 October 31 2025, v2 February 21 2026.

**Problem**: Existing benchmarks for evaluating LLM long-term memory suffer from three limitations: (1) they artificially concatenate short sessions, producing incoherent conversations with abrupt topic shifts, (2) they cover narrow domains and test primarily simple recall, and (3) they max out at ~1.5M tokens, far below the emerging 10M+ context windows.

**Core contribution**: Two things. First, **BEAM**, a benchmark of 100 coherent, topically diverse conversations spanning 100K to 10M tokens with 2,000 human-validated probing questions across 10 memory abilities. Second, **LIGHT**, a cognitively-inspired memory framework combining episodic retrieval, working memory, and an accumulative scratchpad. LIGHT achieves 3.5%-12.69% average improvement over strongest baselines, with gains up to +155.7% at 10M tokens.

**Scale**: 100 conversations (20 at 128K, 35 at 500K, 35 at 1M, 10 at 10M tokens). 2,000 validated probing questions (20 per conversation, 2 per ability). Diverse domains: general, coding, mathematical topics across 15 domain categories. Four models evaluated (Qwen2.5-32B-AWQ, Llama-4-Maverick-fp8, Gemini 2.0-Flash, GPT-4.1-nano) across three methods (Vanilla long-context, RAG, LIGHT).

**Code and data**: MIT (code), CC BY-SA 4.0 (data). GitHub: https://github.com/mohammadtavakoli78/BEAM.

## 2. Benchmark Design

### The Ten Memory Abilities

| # | Ability | Definition | Questions |
|---|---------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | Abstention | Withhold answers when evidence is absent from conversation | 200 |
| 2 | Contradiction Resolution | Detect and reconcile inconsistent statements within conversation | 200 |
| 3 | Event Ordering | Recognize correct sequence of evolving information | 200 |
| 4 | Information Extraction | Factual detail recall (entity, event, attribute) | 200 |
| 5 | Instruction Following | Sustained adherence to user-specified constraints over long span | 200 |
| 6 | Knowledge Update | Revise facts as new/corrected information appears | 200 |
| 7 | Multi-hop Reasoning | Integrate evidence dispersed across non-adjacent segments | 200 |
| 8 | Preference Following | Personalized adaptive responses based on accumulated user preferences | 200 |
| 9 | Summarization | Abstract and condense dialogue content | 200 |
| 10 | Temporal Reasoning | Reason about time relations (before/after, duration, recency) | 200 |

Each conversation contains exactly 20 probing questions (2 per ability), yielding 2,000 total across 100 conversations.

### Dataset Construction Pipeline

The benchmark uses a multi-stage generation pipeline to produce *coherent, single-user conversations* — a critical distinction from benchmarks that concatenate independent sessions:

1. **Conversation Plans**: Researchers create high-level narratives with user profiles (including MBTI personality types, demographics), relationship graphs, and timelines spanning 15-20 storyline threads per conversation. For 10M-token conversations, ten interlocking plans are generated using either:
   - *Sequential expansion*: successive temporal events maintaining narrative continuity
   - *Hierarchical decomposition*: distinct topical segments spanning the full storyline

2. **User Utterances**: Generated by conditioning LLMs on conversation seeds, prior context, and current dialogue batches. Uses LLaMA-3.3 70B for narrative generation.

3. **Assistant Responses**: Created iteratively with role-playing setup, including question-detection and follow-up modules to ensure bidirectional, realistic interactions. Uses Qwen2.5-32B-AWQ.

4. **Probing Questions**: Auto-generated using GPT-4.1/GPT-4.1-mini, then human-validated. A human evaluator reviews candidates and selects those that are valid and consistent with the conversation. Inter-annotator agreement reported in appendix.

**Human quality evaluation** (5-point Likert scale): Coherence 4.53, Realism 4.57, Complexity 4.64. These are strong scores — the conversations are rated as genuinely coherent, not artifactually stitched.

### Evaluation Metrics

**Primary**: Nugget-based evaluation. Each question comes with atomic semantic criteria ("nuggets"). An LLM judge scores each nugget as 0 (unsatisfied), 0.5 (partially satisfied), or 1 (fully satisfied). Final score is the average across nuggets.

**Exception**: Event Ordering uses Kendall tau-b coefficient, which measures both order fidelity and presence. An LLM equivalence detector aligns events before calculation.

This is more granular than LongMemEval's binary QA accuracy or MemoryAgentBench's SubEM/accuracy metrics. Nugget evaluation allows partial credit, which is more informative for abilities like summarization and multi-hop reasoning where answers exist on a spectrum.

### Conversation Distribution

| Length | Conversations | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| 128K tokens | 20 | Within most models' context windows |
| 500K tokens | 35 | Exceeds most open-source model limits |
| 1M tokens | 35 | At the frontier of current context windows |
| 10M tokens | 10 | Beyond all current model context windows |

The 10M tier is where BEAM enters entirely novel territory — no prior benchmark operates at this scale.

## 3. Key Claims and Evidence

### Claim 1: Long-context LLMs degrade catastrophically at scale, even within their stated context windows.
**Evidence**: At 100K tokens (well within Qwen2.5's 128K window), vanilla performance averages 0.280 across abilities. At 1M tokens with Gemini 2.0-Flash (1M context), vanilla scores 0.193. The degradation is not merely linear — it accelerates.

**Evidence quality**: Strong. The coherent single-user conversations eliminate the confound of topic-shift artifacts. The degradation reflects genuine long-range reasoning failure, not navigational confusion from incoherent inputs.

**Implication for claude-memory**: Even models with million-token windows cannot reliably use them for memory tasks. External memory systems remain necessary.

### Claim 2: LIGHT's three-memory architecture consistently improves performance across models and scales.
**Evidence**: Average improvement of 3.5%-12.69% depending on backbone LLM. At 10M tokens (where vanilla baselines cannot process full context): +155.7% (Llama-Maverick), +107.3% (GPT-4.1-nano). The improvements are consistent across all four models tested.

Key per-ability gains:
- Summarization: +160.6%
- Preference Following: +76.5%
- Information Extraction: +56.7%
- Temporal Reasoning: +56.3%
- Multi-hop Reasoning: +27.2%

Weakest results: Contradiction Resolution remains near-zero across all methods (0.025-0.037 for Qwen2.5 at 100K). This is the hardest ability universally.

**Evidence quality**: Moderate-strong. Four models tested, but all are in the "efficient" tier (32B quantized, nano, flash). No evaluation on frontier-scale models (GPT-4.1 full, Claude Opus, Gemini Ultra). The improvements at 10M tokens are impressive but partially reflect the baseline's near-complete failure rather than LIGHT's excellence.

### Claim 3: All three LIGHT components become essential at 10M-token scale.
**Evidence**: Ablation study at different scales:

| Component Removed | 100K Impact | 1M Impact | 10M Impact |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Retrieval (episodic) | -0.8% | +0.2% | **-8.5%** |
| Scratchpad | -1.1% | -1.4% | **-3.7%** |
| Noise filtering | -2.2% | -2.8% | **-8.3%** |
| Working memory | -1.6% | +0.3% | **-5.7%** |

At 100K, the components have marginal individual impact. At 10M, every component is critical — removing any one causes 3.7-8.5% degradation. Noise filtering is as important as retrieval itself.

**Evidence quality**: Strong for the architectural insight. The scale-dependent necessity of multi-component memory is well-demonstrated. The noise filtering result is particularly actionable: at scale, retrieval quality (precision) matters as much as retrieval capability (recall).

### Claim 4: Retrieval budget has a sweet spot at k=15.
**Evidence**: Performance peaks at k=15 documents retrieved: +7.39% (100K), +10.75% (500K), +6.79% (1M), +6.3% (10M). At k=20, performance *degrades* due to noisy context — confirming the "lost in the middle" phenomenon.

**Evidence quality**: Moderate. The k=15 sweet spot is specific to LIGHT's architecture (BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5 embeddings, FAISS retrieval). The principle (there exists an optimal retrieval budget beyond which noise hurts) is general; the specific number is not.

### Claim 5: Contradiction Resolution is universally catastrophic.
**Evidence**: Near-zero scores across all models and methods (0.025-0.037 range for Qwen2.5 at 100K). Even LIGHT, which improves most abilities, barely moves the needle on contradictions.

**Evidence quality**: Strong, and consistent with MemoryAgentBench's finding that multi-hop selective forgetting maxes at 7%. This is now independently replicated across two ICLR 2026 benchmarks using completely different methodologies. Contradiction handling is confirmed as the hardest unsolved problem in LLM memory.

## 4. Standout Feature

What distinguishes BEAM from the three prior benchmarks:

**vs. LongMemEval**: Scale and coherence. LongMemEval maxes at ~1.5M tokens (LongMemEval_M) and constructs conversations via needle-in-a-haystack embedding of evidence sessions within padding sessions (ShareGPT, UltraChat). BEAM generates coherent, single-narrative conversations up to 10M tokens. The ability taxonomy is also broader: BEAM adds Instruction Following, Preference Following, Event Ordering, Summarization, and Contradiction Resolution beyond LongMemEval's five abilities.

**vs. MemoryAgentBench**: Ecological validity. MemoryAgentBench uses "inject once, query multiple times" with 512-token chunks from existing datasets (RULER/HELMET, MQUAKE). BEAM generates conversations that a real user might actually have — with MBTI-typed personas, relationship graphs, and evolving narratives. MemoryAgentBench tests the memory *system*; BEAM tests the memory *experience*.

**vs. LoCoMo**: Scale by three orders of magnitude. LoCoMo has 10 conversations averaging 9K tokens (~26K max). BEAM's smallest conversations (128K) are 5x LoCoMo's largest. LoCoMo's full-context baseline achieves 72.9% because the conversations fit in a context window — BEAM's 10M-token conversations make full-context impossible, forcing genuine memory system evaluation.

**The unique contribution**: BEAM is the first benchmark where full-context baselines *physically cannot run* on the largest conversations. This forces evaluation of actual memory architectures rather than just long-context attention patterns. The 10M-token tier is adversarial by construction — it tests whether a system can manage information across a scale that no current model can hold in attention.

## 5. Competency Coverage Ratings

### 5a. Ratings for claude-memory's evaluation needs

| Dimension | Coverage | Justification |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| **Information Retrieval** | 90% | "Information Extraction" directly tests factual recall. 200 questions. Nugget-based partial credit. Comprehensively covered. Slightly below 100% because it tests only recall from conversation, not recall from a separately-maintained memory store. |
| **Multi-Session Reasoning** | 75% | "Multi-hop Reasoning" tests integrating dispersed evidence. However, BEAM conversations are continuous single sessions, not multi-session. The dispersion is spatial (non-adjacent segments), not temporal (separate sessions). LongMemEval's MR category is more directly multi-session. |
| **Knowledge Update/Contradiction** | 85% | Both "Knowledge Update" and "Contradiction Resolution" are tested with 200 questions each. This is the most comprehensive coverage of these abilities across all four benchmarks. The near-zero contradiction scores confirm this is genuinely hard. |
| **Temporal Reasoning** | 80% | "Temporal Reasoning" and "Event Ordering" together provide 400 questions on temporal abilities. Uses Kendall tau-b for ordering (good). However, temporal reasoning here operates within a single narrative timeline, not across real calendar dates with session-boundary timestamps. |
| **Abstention/Confidence** | 70% | "Abstention" tests withholding answers when evidence is absent (200 questions). Abstention shows the *strongest* baseline performance, suggesting the questions may be somewhat easier than real-world abstention challenges. No confidence calibration testing (knowing *how certain* you are, not just *whether to answer*). |
| **Write-Path Behavior** | 5% | BEAM does not evaluate how information is stored, indexed, or organized. It tests read-time abilities only. The LIGHT framework does describe write-path behavior (episodic KV extraction, scratchpad accumulation) but the benchmark does not evaluate write quality independently. |
| **Consolidation Quality** | 10% | "Summarization" ability (200 questions) is tangentially related — it tests whether a model can abstract dialogue content. But this tests summarization-as-output, not summarization-as-consolidation (whether stored summaries improve downstream retrieval/reasoning). No evaluation of merge, deduplicate, or compress operations. |
| **Proactive/Contextual Recall** | 40% | "Preference Following" (200 questions) tests whether systems apply accumulated user preferences without being explicitly asked. "Instruction Following" tests sustained constraint adherence. These are forms of proactive recall — the system must remember and apply without being queried. But there is no test of unprompted surfacing of relevant memories. |
| **Relationship/Graph Reasoning** | 15% | Multi-hop Reasoning requires connecting dispersed evidence, which is graph-adjacent. But BEAM does not test entity relationships, co-reference resolution, or graph traversal explicitly. The conversation plans include "relationship graphs" but the probing questions don't specifically test graph-structured knowledge. |
| **Agentic Task Performance** | 20% | "Instruction Following" tests sustained constraint adherence, which is relevant to agentic performance. But BEAM evaluates conversational QA, not tool use, planning, or task execution. MemoryAgentBench's TTL (Test-Time Learning) and LRU (Long-Range Understanding) are more agentic. |

**Aggregate coverage**: 49%. Strong on retrieval and temporal abilities, weak on write-path, consolidation, graph reasoning, and agentic tasks.

### 5b. Ten-Ability Taxonomy Comparison

| BEAM (10 abilities) | LongMemEval (5 abilities) | MemoryAgentBench (4 competencies) | Coverage overlap |
|---------------------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|-----------------|
| Information Extraction | IE (Information Extraction) | AR (Accurate Retrieval) | Full three-way overlap. All benchmarks test basic factual recall. |
| Multi-hop Reasoning | MR (Multi-Session Reasoning) | AR (multi-hop variant) | Strong overlap but different framing. LongMemEval requires cross-session; BEAM requires cross-segment; MAB includes both. |
| Knowledge Update | KU (Knowledge Updates) | SF (Selective Forgetting) | Full three-way overlap. All test revision of stored facts. |
| Temporal Reasoning | TR (Temporal Reasoning) | -- | Two-way overlap (BEAM + LongMemEval). MAB has no explicit temporal category. |
| Abstention | ABS (Abstention) | -- | Two-way overlap (BEAM + LongMemEval). MAB does not test abstention. |
| Contradiction Resolution | -- | SF (partial: conflict detection) | Novel emphasis in BEAM. LongMemEval folds this into KU. MAB's SF focuses on overwrite, not detection of contradiction. |
| Event Ordering | -- | -- | **BEAM-only**. Neither prior benchmark explicitly tests event sequencing with order-fidelity metrics. |
| Instruction Following | -- | TTL (partial: constraint adherence) | BEAM-only as a named ability. MAB's TTL is broader (test-time learning). |
| Preference Following | -- | -- | **BEAM-only**. Tests accumulated personalization. No direct equivalent in prior benchmarks. |
| Summarization | -- | LRU (partial: summarization task) | Partial overlap. MAB includes InfinityBench summarization under LRU. LongMemEval does not test summarization. |
| -- | -- | TTL (Test-Time Learning) | **MAB-only**. Few-shot learning during deployment. Neither BEAM nor LongMemEval test this. |
| -- | -- | LRU (Long-Range Understanding) | **MAB-only** as a category. Holistic comprehension vs. snippet extraction. BEAM's summarization partially overlaps. |

**Taxonomy assessment**: BEAM has the broadest ability taxonomy (10 vs. 5 vs. 4). It uniquely adds Event Ordering, Instruction Following, and Preference Following. However, it lacks TTL (test-time learning) and does not capture LRU's "holistic understanding" framing. The three benchmarks together cover approximately 13-14 distinct abilities, with core retrieval/update/temporal reasoning as the consensus overlap.

## 6. Relevance to claude-memory

### Could we run against BEAM?

**Partially, with significant adaptation.** BEAM evaluates conversational QA — the system reads a long conversation and answers probing questions about it. claude-memory is a persistent memory system that incrementally stores and retrieves information across sessions. The evaluation paradigms are fundamentally different:

- **BEAM assumes**: A single long conversation is provided (or retrieved from) at inference time. The "memory" challenge is managing information within or from this conversation.
- **claude-memory assumes**: Information arrives incrementally across sessions. The challenge is deciding what to store, how to index it, and how to retrieve it later.

**Adaptation path**: We could adapt BEAM by treating each conversation as a stream of `remember()` calls (storing salient facts from each turn) and then evaluating recall quality on the probing questions. This would test:
- Whether our extraction pipeline captures the right information (write-path quality)
- Whether our retrieval returns relevant memories for each question type (read-path quality)
- Whether our decay/consolidation preserves important facts over long "time" spans (simulated by conversation length)

The 10M-token conversations would require thousands of `remember()` calls, which would stress-test our SQLite + sqlite-vec infrastructure at a scale we haven't reached (386 memories currently, likely 2,000-5,000 for a single 10M-token conversation).

### What would it reveal?

1. **Extraction coverage**: What fraction of BEAM's probing question answers are captured by our `remember()` extraction? This is currently our blind spot — we have no systematic evaluation of what gets stored.
2. **Retrieval precision at scale**: With 2,000-5,000 memories from a single conversation, how does our RRF fusion perform? Our k=14 RRF parameter was tuned on 386 memories. Scaling behavior is unknown.
3. **Contradiction handling**: BEAM's near-zero baseline on Contradiction Resolution would give us a clear target. If we can beat zero, we're ahead of every evaluated system.
4. **Temporal reasoning**: Our memories have `created_at` timestamps but no `valid_from`/`valid_until` yet. BEAM's temporal reasoning questions would reveal whether timestamp-only temporal reasoning is sufficient.
5. **Preference Following**: This maps directly to our use case — does the system remember and apply user preferences without being explicitly reminded?

### Effort estimate

- **Low**: Download dataset, inspect format, run probing questions against a full-context baseline (~1 day)
- **Medium**: Build adapter to stream conversations into `remember()` calls, evaluate retrieval against probing questions (~3-5 days)
- **High**: Full evaluation pipeline with per-ability scoring, ablations, parameter tuning (~2 weeks)

The medium path is the sweet spot for our needs. It would reveal extraction and retrieval quality without requiring the full LIGHT framework implementation.

## 7. Insights Worth Stealing

Ranked by effort/impact ratio (best first):

### 7.1 Nugget-based evaluation (High impact, Low effort)
BEAM's nugget evaluation (atomic semantic criteria, scored 0/0.5/1 by LLM judge) is strictly better than binary QA accuracy for partial-credit abilities. We should adopt this for any custom evaluation we build. Each question decomposes into nuggets; each nugget is independently scored. This naturally handles multi-fact answers and partial recall.

**Adaptation**: When building CMA behavioral probes (#10 on priority list), use nugget decomposition rather than binary correct/incorrect.

### 7.2 Noise filtering as a first-class component (High impact, Low effort)
LIGHT's ablation shows noise filtering is as important as retrieval at 10M scale (-8.3% without it vs. -8.5% without retrieval). Their approach: semantically chunk scratchpad content, classify each chunk as relevant/irrelevant to the current query, include only relevant chunks.

**Adaptation**: Our `recall()` returns top-k by RRF score. We could add a post-RRF relevance filter — a lightweight LLM call (or even a cosine threshold) that drops retrieved memories below a relevance threshold before presenting to the main model. Our existing QUALITY_FLOOR_RATIO (0.3) is a step in this direction, but it's score-based rather than content-based. A content-relevance filter would catch memories that score well on keyword/embedding match but are semantically tangential.

### 7.3 Scratchpad-style accumulative facts (Medium impact, Medium effort)
LIGHT's scratchpad extracts salient facts after each turn and accumulates them, compressing at 30K tokens to 15K tokens. This is structurally similar to our `remember()` pipeline but with two key differences: (a) it operates on every turn automatically, not selectively, and (b) it compresses rather than discards when hitting limits.

**Adaptation**: Our auto-capture via hooks already moves toward automatic extraction. The compression insight is more novel — instead of just dropping low-priority memories during decay, we could add a consolidation compression pass that merges similar memories into denser representations. This is what NREM sleep partially does, but LIGHT's continuous compression (30K -> 15K threshold) is a lighter-weight version that could operate at `remember()` time.

### 7.4 Retrieval budget optimization methodology (Medium impact, Low effort)
BEAM's systematic sweep of k (retrieval budget) from 5 to 20, showing performance peaks at k=15 then degrades, is a useful methodology we should replicate. Our k=14 RRF parameter was set via Bayesian optimization (Experiment 7) but we haven't systematically tested the number of results returned to the LLM.

**Adaptation**: Run a parameter sweep on our `recall()` limit parameter using historical queries and feedback data. The principle — there's a sweet spot, and more retrieval hurts — should inform our default limit.

### 7.5 Scale-dependent component necessity (Medium impact, informational)
The ablation showing that components have marginal impact at 100K but become essential at 10M is a useful architectural planning insight. As our memory store grows (currently 386, eventually thousands), we should expect currently-marginal features (shadow load, confidence scoring) to become load-bearing.

**Adaptation**: No direct implementation, but this validates our investment in features that currently have minimal measurable impact. Shadow load's quadratic penalty (0.005 * shadow^2) may become critical at 2,000+ memories even though it barely affects retrieval at 386.

### 7.6 Preference Following as evaluation category (Low impact, Low effort)
BEAM's explicit "Preference Following" ability is directly relevant to our use case. We should include preference-based probing questions in any custom evaluation, testing whether the system remembers and applies user preferences across sessions.

**Adaptation**: Add preference-following questions to CMA behavioral probes (#10).

### 7.7 Event Ordering with Kendall tau-b (Low impact, Medium effort)
Using Kendall tau-b coefficient for ordering evaluation is more principled than binary correct/incorrect for sequence tasks. It captures partial ordering accuracy.

**Adaptation**: If we build temporal reasoning evaluations, adopt Kendall tau-b for any sequence ordering tasks.

## 8. What's Not Worth It

### LIGHT framework implementation
LIGHT's architecture (episodic retrieval + working memory + scratchpad) is designed for single-session inference over a long conversation. Our system operates across sessions with persistent storage. The scratchpad concept has transferable elements (see 7.3) but implementing LIGHT wholesale would be solving a different problem.

### 10M-token conversation generation pipeline
The multi-stage pipeline (conversation plans with MBTI profiles + narrative generation + assistant responses + probing questions) is an impressive data engineering effort but irrelevant to our needs. We don't need to generate test conversations at this scale — our real conversations *are* our data.

### Embedding model choice (BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5)
LIGHT uses bge-small-en-v1.5 (384-dim). We use text-embedding-3-small (also 384-dim when configured). The embedding model choice is a hyperparameter specific to their infrastructure, not a finding.

### Domain diversity analysis
BEAM spans "general, coding, and mathematical topics across 15 domain categories." Our use case is single-user with a specific domain profile (memory research, personal projects, creative work). Domain diversity is a benchmark design consideration, not a system design consideration.

## 9. Key Takeaway

BEAM's most important contribution to our work is empirical confirmation that contradiction resolution is universally catastrophic — now independently replicated across two ICLR 2026 benchmarks (BEAM and MemoryAgentBench) using completely different methodologies. This validates our prioritization of graded contradiction detection (#3) as a high-value differentiator. The noise filtering result is the most directly actionable finding: at scale, *precision in what you surface* matters as much as recall. Our post-RRF quality floor mechanism is a crude version of this, but a content-aware relevance filter could yield the 8.3% improvement BEAM demonstrates. The benchmark itself is too oriented toward single-session conversational QA to evaluate our system directly, but its ability taxonomy (especially Preference Following and Event Ordering) and nugget-based evaluation methodology are worth adopting for our custom evaluation design.

## 10. Impact on Implementation Priority

**No reordering of the #1-#14 priority list.** Specific impacts:

- **#3 (Contradiction detection)**: Urgency reinforced. BEAM adds a third independent data point (after MemoryAgentBench and the RL papers) confirming contradiction handling is universally unsolved. Near-zero scores across all methods, all models. Our `valid_from`/`valid_until` approach is still the right direction, but BEAM reveals that even *detecting* contradictions (not just resolving them) is near-impossible for current systems. Consider: a contradiction detection stage before resolution — can we even flag when two memories contradict?

- **#10 (CMA behavioral probes)**: Enriched. BEAM's ability taxonomy adds three evaluation categories we should incorporate: (a) Preference Following (does the system apply accumulated preferences?), (b) Event Ordering (does it maintain temporal sequence?), (c) Instruction Following (does it respect long-lived constraints?). BEAM's nugget-based evaluation methodology should be adopted for partial-credit scoring in our probes.

- **#12 (Reading strategy optimization)**: Reinforced. BEAM's noise filtering ablation (-8.3% without filtering at 10M) confirms that post-retrieval relevance filtering is high-leverage. Our QUALITY_FLOOR_RATIO is score-based; a content-relevance filter would be complementary.

- **#1 (RRF fusion)**: Validated but no change. BEAM's LIGHT uses single-channel vector retrieval (FAISS + bge-small), not multi-channel fusion. The fact that even single-channel retrieval + noise filtering achieves strong results does not undermine multi-channel RRF — it confirms that retrieval quality (noise filtering) is at least as important as retrieval breadth (multi-channel). Our three-channel target (vector + FTS5 + PPR) remains correct; we should add content-aware noise filtering as a post-fusion step.

- **#5 (Sleep/consolidation)**: LIGHT's scratchpad compression (30K -> 15K threshold) is a lighter-weight complement to our batch consolidation in NREM sleep. Write-time compression could reduce the consolidation burden. Filed as a consideration, not a priority change.

### Cross-benchmark synthesis

The four benchmarks together form a complementary evaluation landscape:

| Dimension | LoCoMo (2024) | LongMemEval (2025) | MAB (2026) | BEAM (2026) |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|------------|-------------|
| Max scale | 26K tokens | 1.5M tokens | 1.44M tokens | 10M tokens |
| Conversations | 10 | 500 questions | 146 instances | 100 conversations |
| Ability taxonomy | 5 types | 5 abilities | 4 competencies | 10 abilities |
| Construction | Machine-human | Needle-in-haystack | Inject-once-query-many | Coherent narrative |
| Ecological validity | Medium | Medium-low | Low | High |
| Metric | LLM-judge binary | LLM-judge typed | SubEM/accuracy | Nugget partial credit |
| Write-path eval | No | No | No | No |

The glaring shared gap: **no benchmark evaluates write-path behavior**. All four test retrieval and reading. None tests whether the system stored the right things in the right way. This remains our opportunity for a differentiated custom evaluation.
